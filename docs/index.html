Is your representative
actually representing you?

Gerrymandering and representation distortion are often discussed in the abstract. This project turns those ideas into something concrete: a public-facing way to explore what voters care about, what legislators actually do, and where those two things meaningfully align or break apart.

Content developed from the policy team: Includes defining key democratic values, identifying and explaining the harms of gerrymandering, and situating the project within the broader legal struggles of regulation and representation. This landing page should feel like the front door to that argument: rigorous, public-facing, and grounded in both evidence and civic stakes.

The goal is not simply to say that distortion exists. It is to help people understand where it exists, how it shapes responsiveness, and why the gap between constituent priorities and legislative behavior matters for democratic legitimacy.

Representation is not only about winning elections. It is also about whether institutions respond to public needs, whether district boundaries distort voter voice, and whether legislative agendas map onto what communities actually say they care about. That means evaluating more than party labels or outcomes alone.

This project compares signals from public opinion and issue salience with observable lawmaker behavior. The result is a more concrete way to discuss whether a district is merely electing someone, or whether it is being meaningfully represented.

A useful civic tool should do more than inform. It should help people move from confusion to clarity. That means making methods transparent, showing where measurements come from, and presenting findings in ways that are legible to non-experts without flattening the nuance out of the research.

Visitors should be able to move from broad explanation into case studies, methodology, and district-level exploration with minimal friction. The visual system supports that goal by using soft but intentional emphasis, large editorial hierarchy, and a restrained set of symbolic graphics instead of noisy dashboards.
