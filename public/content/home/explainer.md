# How Does Gerrymandering Harm Our Society?

Gerrymandering harms our society on multiple levels. It influences the policies that directly shape our everyday life, disrupts the alignment between our interests and our represented officials, and fundamentally undermines the values of democracy that our society is built upon.

## Policy and Social Consequences of Gerrymandering

Heavily gerrymandered states gain greater power to pass legislation that could negatively impact the quality of the residents' lives with less consequences, even if it doesn’t align with the residents’ interests at all. Below are some examples of such cases.

### **Voting Access Policy**

After the 2011–12 legislative session, the Brennan Center for Justice estimated that twenty-seven new voting restrictions were passed in eighteen states. Heavily gerrymandered Republican legislatures passed significantly more restrictive voting laws, raising the cost of voting through stricter ID requirements, reduced early voting, and voter roll purges. This disproportionately suppressed turnout among minority, low-income, and younger voters.

### **Health Policy**

Gerrymandered legislatures were less responsive to public health needs, including refusing Medicaid expansion, passing restrictive abortion laws opposed by medical professionals, and adopting fewer energy efficiency and environmental standards.

Since 2011, states enacted over 400 abortion restrictions, with early-term bans passing in competitive states like Michigan, North Carolina, Ohio, and Wisconsin due to gerrymandering.

In Flint, Michigan, a state-appointed manager switched the city's water supply without proper treatment, killing twelve people and causing lead poisoning linked to fetal deaths and lower birth weights.

Researchers of Cambridge University linked these policy choices to measurable health disparities, including higher infant mortality and rising opioid deaths, especially in communities of color.

### **Democratic Accountability**

Most fundamentally, gerrymandering breaks the basic feedback loop of democratic government. Even in states where voters were roughly evenly split between parties, biased maps allowed legislatures to pass policies that polls showed most voters opposed.

Polling in Michigan, North Carolina, Ohio, and Wisconsin showed legislatures acting against majority voter preferences on Medicaid expansion, gun violence prevention, minimum wage increases, and safe drinking water. In Wisconsin, the legislature passed unpopular policies including pension tax increases and right-to-work laws that weakened organized labor.

## **A Self-Reinforcing Cycle**

Gerrymandering is protected by a deliberate cycle: Gerrymanders protect legislative majorities, and those majorities pass policies that harm vulnerable communities, and then voting restrictions make it harder for those same communities to vote the legislators out.
