This project began as a collaboration between the [Political Computer Science Club at UC Berkeley](https://pcs.studentorg.berkeley.edu/) and [Indivisible East Bay](https://indivisibleeb.org/), the East Bay chapter of the national [Indivisible](https://indivisible.org/) movement. The Political Computer Science Club is a student organization focused on exploring the intersection of technology and politics, and Indivisible East Bay reached out to the club to propose a joint project to raise awareness about the tangible impacts of gerrymandering. Rather than producing a traditional research paper or presentation, the team chose to build something interactive: a website that could be easily shared, easily understood, and meet readers where they actually engage with information about politics today.

The goal of the project is twofold. First, we want to raise awareness of how gerrymandering shapes the day-to-day experience of being represented in Congress, by showing concretely how closely (or how poorly) a given representative's voting record matches the policy preferences of the people they represent. Second, we want to give readers something to do with that information. Each state page connects directly to opportunities for civic engagement: contacting representatives, learning about redistricting reform efforts, and getting involved with organizations working on these issues at the state and local level. A score on a website is only useful if it leads to action, and the action is the point.

The team that built this site is a mix of policy & legal researchers, data scientists, and developers, all UC Berkeley undergraduates working through the Political Computer Science Club. Each section of the project, from the underlying data pipeline to the policy explanations to the front-end design, was built collaboratively across the team. The site is open-source; the full codebase is available on [GitHub](https://github.com/Trinity-7142/gerrymandering-project), and we welcome questions, critiques, and contributions from anyone interested in the methodology or the broader project.
