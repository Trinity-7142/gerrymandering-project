Born in Waxahachie, Texas, and raised in public housing in Wichita Falls, Cleaver earned a B.A. from Prairie View A&M University and a Master of Divinity from St. Paul School of Theology, later serving as a United Methodist pastor. He was elected Kansas City's first African American mayor in 1991 before entering Congress in 2005. A progressive Democrat, he has championed legislation to lower housing costs, strengthen Social Security, expand Head Start and Meals on Wheels, support abortion rights, ban assault-style weapons, and raise the federal minimum wage to $15. His Housing Choice Voucher Mobility Demonstration Act, aimed at helping low-income families access better neighborhoods, passed with bipartisan support and was signed into law. A notable controversy involved a Bank of America lawsuit over a defaulted $1.46 million loan; the suit was settled and Cleaver's congressional wages were garnished to repay the debt.
