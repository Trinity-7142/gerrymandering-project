Virginia's congressional delegation held 11 seats following the 2020 reapportionment. Under a constitutional measure (Amendment 1) passed in 2020, congressional lines are drawn by a 16-member bipartisan commission — eight legislators and eight citizens — designed to reflect the state's racial, ethnic, geographic, and gender diversity. For a congressional map to advance, at least six of the eight legislators and six of the eight citizens must vote in favor, a supermajority requirement designed to force bipartisan compromise. In the 2020 cycle, the commission failed to pass plans by the required supermajority, so the state Supreme Court took over and issued congressional and state legislative maps on December 28, 2021. The Court drew on two court-appointed special masters — one nominated by each party. Notably, a 2026 constitutional amendment on the April 21, 2026 ballot would temporarily allow the state legislature to draw congressional districts, with authority reverting to the commission after the 2030 census. If approved, new maps project a 10-to-1 partisan split, with Democrats potentially gaining four additional U.S. House seats.
