Nevada is divided into four congressional districts, each represented by a member of the U.S. House of Representatives. Congressional and state legislative lines are drawn by the legislature as a regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto. During a special session that adjourned on November 16, 2021, the legislature passed Senate Bill 1, which revised the boundaries of all four congressional districts; Governor Steve Sisolak (D) signed the congressional map into law that same day, and it took effect for Nevada's 2022 elections. The maps passed along party lines, and Republicans from the state's rural areas filed suit claiming the maps were drawn to dilute the voting power of rural conservatives. Additionally, members of the Latino community in Las Vegas voiced frustration after the maps split up a predominantly working-class, largely Latino congressional district. Efforts to reform the process have repeatedly stalled: a 2020 ballot initiative to change redistricting failed to gather enough signatures during the pandemic, and a 2024 reform initiative was struck by courts as an unlawful unfunded mandate.
