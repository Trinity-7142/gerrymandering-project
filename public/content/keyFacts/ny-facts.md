New York comprises 26 congressional districts. As of 2014, New York's congressional and state legislative lines are drawn primarily by a 10-person Independent Redistricting Commission (IRC). In 2014, New York voters adopted a constitutional amendment that overhauled the state's redistricting process, creating a bipartisan IRC and explicitly banning partisan gerrymandering. The current congressional map has a turbulent history: the bipartisan commission could not reach agreement on a map for the 2022 election, the legislature then intervened and drew its own map that heavily favored Democrats, but the Court of Appeals blocked it, and a court-appointed special master drew the map used in 2022. The commission submitted new congressional plans to the legislature in February 2024, which the legislature rejected; on February 28, 2024, the legislature passed its own congressional plan, which the governor signed the same day. Most recently, on January 21, 2026, state judge Jeffrey Pearlman ruled that New York's 11th Congressional District unconstitutionally diluted Black and Latino voting power and ordered the state's redistricting commission to redraw the congressional map.
