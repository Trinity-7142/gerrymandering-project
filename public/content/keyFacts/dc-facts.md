Washington, D.C.'s at-large congressional district encompasses the entire federal district. No traditional redistricting process applies, as Article One of the U.S. Constitution limits congressional representation to "States," so the elected member is not permitted to vote on the House floor; instead, residents elect a non-voting delegate. Though the delegate lacks full voting privileges, they may sit on, vote in, and chair congressional committees, join party caucuses, and introduce legislation. The modern delegate office was established in 1971. The most notable ongoing controversy is the question of full representation: a DC Voting Rights Constitutional Amendment passed Congress in 1978 but failed to receive the required state ratifications by its 1985 deadline, and subsequent efforts have largely failed along party lines. The slogan "End Taxation Without Representation," found on DC license plates, highlights one of the primary harms caused by the lack of voting representation.
