Following the 2020 census, Iowa retained four congressional districts. Iowa's congressional and state legislative lines are drawn by the state legislature as a regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto, but with substantial input from the nonpartisan Legislative Services Agency (LSA) — a body of civil servants committed to nonpartisanship. State law specifically highlights factors that must not be considered, including favoritism for a political party, incumbent legislator, or member of Congress. Iowa law also requires that individual counties cannot be split into multiple legislative or congressional districts. On November 4, 2021, Gov. Kim Reynolds signed the current maps into law after the LSA proposed them on October 21, 2021; the legislature approved them 48–1 in the Senate and 93–2 in the House, with lawmakers permitted only to accept or reject — not amend — the maps, which took effect for the 2022 elections. Notably, Iowa Republicans had voted during a special session to reject the LSA's first proposed maps, citing compactness and contiguity concerns, prompting the LSA to submit a revised second proposal — the one ultimately enacted. Unlike most states, Iowa has relied on this nonpartisan LSA-led process since 1980, and it is widely regarded as a national model for nonpartisan redistricting.
