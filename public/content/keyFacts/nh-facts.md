New Hampshire's congressional and state legislative lines are drawn by the legislature, as a regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto. New Hampshire comprises two congressional districts. Following the 2020 census, the current maps were finalized through an unusually contentious process: the legislature passed a congressional plan (HB 52) on March 17, 2022, and, anticipating a veto, passed another (SB 200) on May 26, 2022; the governor vetoed both on May 27, 2022. When the legislature failed to draw congressional lines, the state supreme court found the existing districts malapportioned and adopted a congressional map following a "least change" approach. The court released a unanimous order implementing a map drawn by Nathaniel Persily, a Stanford Law School professor appointed by the court. Notably, voting rights groups criticized the legislature's earlier Republican-backed proposals, accusing Republicans of gerrymandering the congressional districts in their favor. The congressional boundary line, which has not substantially changed in over 100 years, required only minor adjustment of about 9,000 people to equalize population between the two districts.
