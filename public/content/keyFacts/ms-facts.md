Mississippi has 4 congressional districts. Congressional lines are drawn by the legislature as a regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto. A Joint Congressional Redistricting Committee — a 20-member advisory body composed of legislative leaders and members appointed by the Speaker and Lieutenant Governor — proposes a map to the full legislature, which may use, amend, or reject it. In the 2020 cycle, the legislature passed congressional districts (HB 384) on January 12, 2022, which the governor signed on January 24, 2022. The House approved the plan 75–44 and the Senate 33–18, with votes largely along party lines. The new map preserved the existing partisan balance, keeping three Republican seats and one Democratic seat. Notably, this was the first redistricting cycle since the 1965 Voting Rights Act to proceed without federal preclearance oversight, following the Supreme Court's 2013 *Shelby County* decision. At the state legislative level, the Mississippi NAACP sued in December 2022, and a federal three-judge panel ruled in July 2024 that both the House and Senate legislative maps violated Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, ordering the legislature to draw additional majority-Black districts.
