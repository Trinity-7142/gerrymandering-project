Michigan is divided into 13 congressional districts, each represented by a member of the U.S. House of Representatives. In 2018, Michigan voters approved Proposal 2, a citizen-led ballot initiative that created the Michigan Independent Citizens Redistricting Commission (MICRC), which is now responsible for drawing the state's congressional, State Senate, and State House maps. The commission comprises 13 members — four Democrats, four Republicans, and five unaffiliated voters — and requires at least seven votes to enact a map, including at least two from each major party and two unaffiliated members. On December 28, 2021, the MICRC approved the congressional "Chestnut" map by a vote of 8–5, with boundaries becoming law on March 26, 2022. A key controversy followed: challenges to the congressional map were rejected, but a federal court struck down the state legislative maps based on the unjustified predominance of race, specifically finding that thirteen legislative districts violated the 14th Amendment by undermining the voting power of Black and brown Metro Detroit voters.
