In Ohio, congressional district boundaries are set by the state legislature. The district-redrawing process is governed by a 2018 constitutional amendment passed by voters with nearly 75% support, which gives the legislature the first opportunity to draw congressional lines by supermajority; if it fails, a seven-member backup politician commission takes over; lines passed with bipartisan backing are valid for ten years, while lines passed without bipartisan backing are valid for only two general elections. Following the 2020 Census, Ohio lost one congressional seat. The current map, in place since March 2022, was drawn by the Republican-dominated redistricting commission after the Ohio Supreme Court invalidated an earlier version for being a partisan gerrymander. The Ohio Supreme Court twice struck down versions of the state's congressional map for unfairly benefiting Republicans, and on October 31, 2025, the backup commission unanimously passed a new congressional plan valid for the rest of the decade. Voters qualified an initiative for the 2024 ballot to create a more independent redistricting process, but voters ultimately rejected the measure.
