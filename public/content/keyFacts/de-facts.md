Delaware has only one at-large congressional district, meaning the entire state elects a single U.S. House representative and no congressional district boundary-drawing is required. The at-large district has had the same geographical borders since 1789, making it the oldest district in the country that has never changed its shape or size. For state legislative maps, plans are drawn and enacted by the Delaware General Assembly, subject to the governor's veto, which the General Assembly can override with a three-fifths vote in each chamber. Following the 2020 census, the legislature passed a new state legislative plan (SB 199) on November 1, 2021, which the governor signed the next day; a revision (HB 335) was passed on March 23, 2022 and signed on April 1, 2022. Notably, Delaware law prohibits redrawing district lines mid-decade, before the next census. With both chambers and the governorship controlled by one party, critics have noted that single-party control increases the risk of partisan gerrymandering; proposals for an independent redistricting commission have been introduced but not enacted.
