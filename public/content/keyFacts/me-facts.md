Maine has two congressional districts. Both congressional and state legislative district boundaries are drawn by the state legislature, aided by an advisory commission, and a two-thirds majority is required to approve new maps, which are also subject to veto by the governor. If the legislature cannot pass a plan with a two-thirds supermajority of each chamber, responsibility for drawing maps falls to the state Supreme Court. The Maine Apportionment Commission approved a final congressional district plan in September 2021, and the State Senate and House of Representatives unanimously approved the new map on September 29, 2021, taking effect for the 2022 elections. Because of delays in the release of 2020 Census data, the Maine Supreme Judicial Court granted a petition by legislative leaders to extend the state's statutory redistricting timeline, preserving the legislature's map-drawing authority. Notably, Maine has been a national flashpoint over ranked-choice voting (RCV) and representation: RCV is allowed in primaries for governor and the legislature, as well as for all federal races, but the Maine Supreme Court recently ruled that a proposed expansion of RCV to general elections for governor and legislative seats would violate the state constitution, which makes clear that the first candidate to earn a plurality of the vote wins those races.
