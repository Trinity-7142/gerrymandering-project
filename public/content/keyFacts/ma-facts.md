Massachusetts comprises nine congressional districts. Congressional and state legislative lines are drawn by the state legislature as regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto. There are no state law requirements for drawing congressional districts beyond federal standards. Gov. Charlie Baker (R) signed the current congressional maps into law on November 22, 2021, after both chambers approved them on November 17, 2021. The map took effect for Massachusetts' 2022 congressional elections. Redistricting is under effective single-party Democratic control; proposals for an independent redistricting commission were considered in the 2020 legislative session but received no further action before the legislature adjourned. Notably, internal Democratic disagreements drove most debate: one senator opposed the maps, citing the splitting of the MetroWest region "into five different bits" and the bifurcation of Fall River and New Bedford. Massachusetts also holds a unique historical distinction — in 1812, Gov. Elbridge Gerry signed a district map that gave disproportionate representation to his party, and the term "gerrymander" was coined to describe it.
