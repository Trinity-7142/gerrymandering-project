New Mexico has three congressional districts. Congressional lines are drawn by the legislature as a regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto. For the 2020 cycle, the legislature passed SB 304, which the governor signed on April 6, 2021, creating a Citizens Redistricting Committee (CRC) — an advisory body to draw drafts of federal and state lines. The seven-member CRC draws and submits at least three proposals for each type of plan to the legislature, which is free to use, amend, or reject them. The legislature substantially modified the CRC's proposals, passing a new congressional plan (SB 1) on December 11, 2021, which the governor signed on December 17, 2021. The maps proved controversial: a judge found clear evidence that Democratic lawmakers intentionally tried to dilute Republican votes in the 2nd Congressional District, but ruled that the effort, while "substantial," did not "rise to the level of an egregious gerrymander." On November 27, 2023, the New Mexico Supreme Court upheld the congressional map and dismissed remaining litigation against it. A separate study also found that incumbency protection was "the greatest source of gerrymandering" in the session.
