Tennessee comprises nine congressional districts. Congressional and state legislative district lines are drawn by the state legislature as a regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto. The General Assembly can override a veto with a majority vote in each chamber, and Republicans currently hold a veto-proof majority in both chambers. In the 2020 cycle, the legislature passed the congressional plan on January 24, 2022, and the governor signed all maps on February 6, 2022. The Senate Judiciary Committee recommended the congressional plan in a 7–2 party-line vote; the full Senate approved it 26–5, and the House 70–26, both along party lines. A notable controversy was the map's treatment of Davidson County (Nashville): under the enacted congressional map, Democrats lost a favorable district, leaving the state with only one Democrat-leaning congressional district and eight that favor Republicans. Critics alleged that the Republican-controlled legislature diluted the voting power of Black residents through redistricting around Memphis and Nashville, though a federal court rejected challenges alleging unconstitutional use of race and intentional racial discrimination.
