Connecticut comprises five congressional districts. The state legislature is responsible for drawing both congressional and state legislative districts, requiring a supermajority of two-thirds of each chamber to adopt a plan; maps do not require approval by the governor. If the legislature fails to act, a nine-member bipartisan Reapportionment Commission is formed, with each of the four legislative leaders appointing two members, who then jointly select a ninth. On February 10, 2022, Connecticut enacted its current congressional district boundaries when the state Supreme Court adopted the plan submitted by court-appointed special master Nathaniel Persily, who had been appointed on December 23, 2021. The court assumed control after the Reapportionment Commission missed an extended deadline, which had itself taken over when the legislature's Reapportionment Committee failed to meet a statutory September 15, 2021, deadline due to delays in census data release. Notably, Governor Ned Lamont signed Public Act No. 21-13 abolishing prison gerrymandering in Connecticut, addressing a longstanding controversy in which the NAACP and five Connecticut voters had previously sued, alleging that counting incarcerated people at their facility rather than their pre-incarceration residence violated the Equal Protection Clause.
