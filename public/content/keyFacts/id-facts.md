Idaho comprises two congressional districts. Both congressional and legislative plans are drawn and adopted by Idaho's Citizen Commission for Reapportionment, a six-member politically appointed body, with members appointed by the President Pro Tempore of the Idaho Senate, the Speaker of the Idaho House, the minority leaders in both chambers, and the state chairmen of the two largest political parties. Until 1986, the Idaho Legislature was responsible for redistricting; voters then approved constitutional amendments creating this bipartisan commission process. Following the 2020 census, the commission adopted Congressional Plan C03 on November 5, 2021, reaffirmed the vote on November 10, and filed its final report with the Secretary of State on November 12. The congressional map passed 4–2, while the legislative map passed 6–0. A notable point of contention was the continued splitting of Ada County between the two districts; two dissenting commissioners cited the splitting of Ada County as one of their reasons for opposing the congressional map. The maps also faced multiple court challenges: one challenge alleged the legislative boundaries improperly split the reservations of the Shoshone-Bannock Tribes and Coeur d'Alene Tribe, but the Idaho Supreme Court ultimately upheld the congressional plan on February 11, 2022, leaving the commission's map in effect beginning with the 2022 elections.
