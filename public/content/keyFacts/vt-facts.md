As of the 2020 Census, Vermont has only one at-large congressional district, meaning the state's sole U.S. House representative is elected statewide. Vermont was apportioned one single at-large U.S. House seat, making congressional redistricting after the 2020 census unnecessary. For state legislative maps, Vermont's state legislative lines are drawn by the state legislature, as a regular statute, subject to gubernatorial veto. A seven-member advisory Legislative Apportionment Board makes recommendations to the legislature; the governor appoints one commissioner from each major party, those parties' chairs each appoint one more, and the Chief Justice of the state Supreme Court appoints the chair. On March 30, 2022, the General Assembly passed H.722, reapportioning the House and Senate; it was signed by the governor on April 6, 2022. A notable feature of Vermont's process is that state statutes allow for the protection of incumbents in redistricting, and in the 2011 cycle, lawmakers resisted the advisory commission's plans in part due to concerns over the pairing of incumbents within the same district — a dynamic that critics argue prioritizes lawmakers' security over fair voter representation.
