The Montana Constitution requires an independent five-member commission to draw the political boundaries of congressional and legislative districts every 10 years using population data from the most recent U.S. Census. The commission is composed of two Republicans, two Democrats, and a tiebreaker chair appointed by the Montana Supreme Court. Following the 2020 Census, Montana was apportioned two congressional districts for the first time since the 1990 Census. On November 12, 2021, the Montana Districting and Apportionment Commission (MDAC) enacted a new congressional map, which took effect for Montana's 2022 congressional elections. The commissioners voted 3–2 to approve the map, with both Republican commissioners and nonpartisan chair Maylinn Smith voting in favor, while the two Democratic commissioners voted against it. The Democratic dissent was notable: Democratic Commissioner Kendra Miller argued that "neither district on this plan is seriously competitive," that the two districts together did not adequately represent the state's makeup, and that the plan had been drawn to unduly favor one political party. Additionally, the Montana Legislature has at various points passed laws intended to steer the commission's work, though many of those laws have been overturned by judges who ruled they conflict with the independence guaranteed to the commission by the Montana Constitution.
