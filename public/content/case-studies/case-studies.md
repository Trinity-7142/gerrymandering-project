# “But It’s Just Land?” : Geography as a Sneaky Suppression

## Case of Texas in the 2020 Primary Election

Combining iron and carbon together creates a stronger material, steel; Turns out gerrymandering can do the same. Combined with the closing of poll locations, it can create an incredibly strong double-factor oppression. This is geography used as a tool of political suppression. Let’s take a look at the 2020 Primary Election of Texas, where it is best illustrated.

## **Tradition of Ethnic Gerrymandering**

Texas did not arrive at its 2020 moment by accident. Polling station closure has a storied and infamous history in the United States, particularly prevalent in Southern states, where it has been employed over several decades as a means to restrict voting access in predominantly Black communities, both before and after the Civil Rights Movement, as a form of racial discrimination.

Texas is the clearest heir to this tradition. Gerrymandering has been its preferred instrument for decades. In the Dallas-Fort Worth area, the shape of the districts tells the story plainly. District 24, gerrymandered to favor a Republican Party victory, stretches between the cities of Dallas and Fort Worth but does not include parts of either municipality. Instead, it circles the two cities on their northern sides and encompasses both cities' wealthier, whiter suburbs, successfully neutralizing urban minority votes.

Meanwhile, the districts actually containing those urban populations were drawn in the opposite direction: Districts 30 and 33 both represent the same type of gerrymandering common to urban areas of the United States: Packing. It draws a line around clustered minority communities, concentrating their vote into as few seats as possible.

This was the map Texas entered 2020 with. One already engineered to dilute the political power of its most rapidly growing communities.

## **COVID-19 Decreases Voting Access**

Into this already constrained landscape came COVID-19. The pandemic created an urgent and obvious case for expanding mail-in voting access. Texas did the opposite.

Polling station closure due to COVID was detrimental to voting participation. The stations that remain open become busier, forcing voters to wait longer, sometimes for several hours. When many voters, particularly those in the working class, cannot take time off work, this necessity of standing in line renders it difficult to make the time commitment necessary. Similarly, closing polling stations increases the average distance someone must travel. Millions of voters do not have access to reliable transportation to travel longer distances, making it realistically impossible for them to exercise their voting right.

But rather than expanding mail-in voting as a safety valve during the pandemic, Texas refused to do so. Texas only allows voting by mail for those who are 65 or older, have a disability or illness, are incarcerated, will be out of their county, or are expected to give birth within three weeks of Election Day. During the 2020 election, Texas refused to expand eligibility for mail-in voting despite concerns about exposure to COVID-19. The door to a safer alternative was shut, and the door to in-person voting was growing narrower. Of greatest concern was the possibility that targeted polling station closures had ulterior political motives masqueraded as public health protection.

## **The 2020 Primary: Both Factors in the Same Space**

With polling stations closed because of the pandemic and Texas having gerrymandered districts, it provides a window into the compounding effect on votes geographically and illustrates the two-factor obstacle of voter suppression.

The study *Gerrymandering and Polling Station Closure in Texas Primaries: Two-factor Voter Suppression?*(DiBell et al.) inspects this with detail. The study utilizes polling location data for two elections in Texas: The March primary and its July runoff election. It focuses on the four districts in the Dallas-Fort Worth metropolitan area, analyzing spatially whether polling stations closed between the two elections.

What the spatial analysis found was striking. In District 24(gerrymandered to favor a Republican Party victory) there were closures across the district, with one particularly alarming cluster in the central-north portion, where seven stations were seemingly replaced by only one station in the second election.

In the packed minority districts, the pattern repeated. In District 30, the largely African American district that encompasses the southern side of Dallas gerrymandered in favor of a Democratic candidate, there were 312 polling stations during the March primary, and closures occurred across the entire district by the July runoff, similar to the pattern in District 24. The closures were not partisan in the narrow sense. They hit across districts. But they hit communities that had already been geographically corralled by gerrymandering, meaning each closed station fell inside a boundary that had already been drawn to limit those voters' influence.

The two factors were creating a double suppression here. A voter in a packed minority district who already faced diluted representation now also faced longer lines, fewer stations, greater distances to travel, and no mail ballot option to fall back on.

## **Invisible Under the Cloak of “Geography”**

What makes this form of suppression particularly difficult to challenge is its invisibility. Neither gerrymandering nor polling place closure explicitly names race or party. Both hide behind the neutral language of geography, logistics, and resource allocation. Using similar logic as gerrymandering, politicians have the ability to gauge how certain communities in a given geographic area will vote. And with this information, they can close stations in targeted ways. If these closures are made for political rather than practical reasons, their effects are even more nefarious.

The burden shifts entirely to the people most affected by these changes to monitor changes and advocate for them to be rolled back. By the time a legal challenge is mounted, the election is over.

# California’s 13th district: The Heated Battle of Redistricting

One of the most competitive congressional districts in the US is California's 13th district. This district is located in the San Joaquin Valley, comprising all of Merced County, most of the population of Madera County, and parts of Stanislaus, Fresno, and San Joaquin counties.

## **A Swing District with Heightened Conflicts**

The history of the 13th district is definitely something; it was one of the 13 congressional districts that voted for Donald Trump in the 2024 presidential election while simultaneously electing a Democrat in the 2024 House of Representatives elections. In one sentence, it is a massive swing district.

This paradox is very much baked into the district’s identity. With around 65 percent of the population being Hispanic and a median household income of around $68,434, it is an area defined by agriculture and water rights. The voters tend to be practically driven, rather than ideologically loyal. Their major interests lie on water access, farm policy, and local infrastructure.

Perhaps its election results are what are most alarming about this district. In 2022, Republican John Duarte defeated Democrat Adam Gray by just 564 votes, being the second-closest House race in the country that year. While it should be noted that Duarte’s victory was the first for a Republican in the district since 1974, it still reflects the voting tendencies that are cutthroat. The 2024 race at its core was a rematch from 2022, when Democrat Gray defeated Republican Duarte by less than 200 votes; It was almost a 50 to 50 split. This made the district the most evenly divided congressional seat in the US.

## **A Fight for Drawing the Map**

Given this, it would be no surprise that California’s 13th district was one of the biggest, most heated battlegrounds for redistricting in California ahead of the 2026 elections.

The big context was essentially the clash of the Texas Republicans and the California Democrats. In the summer of 2025, Texas Republicans launched a rare, mid-decade redistricting in the state. The US conducts a national population count called the ‘census’ every ten years. As of 2026, the next one is scheduled for 2030. After each census, districts are redrawn to reflect shifts in population. Since this 10-year cycle is the default, the Texas Republicans’ redistricting was an uncommon case.

The goal was to add five Republican congressional seats ahead of the 2026 elections. The thinking was that the midterm election nationwide was going to be highly competitive, and that adding five seats in Texas may prove to be the margin of victory needed to maintain a House majority.

Corresponding to this movement, California Democrats began taking action toward its own congressional redistricting ahead of the 2026 elections: Proposition 50, widely known as ‘Prop 50.’ Under normal circumstances, California's maps are drawn by the independent Citizens Redistricting Commission, a body created specifically to remove elected politicians from the mapmaking process. Proposition 50 replaced those commission-drawn maps with new, legislatively drawn maps, to remain in use until the commission resumes its role following the 2030 census.

The map defined in Prop 50 is a Democratic gerrymander, claimed to intend to offset the Republican gerrymander in Texas. It redraws several congressional districts to incorporate larger shares of urban and suburban Democratic voters, increasing Democratic registration advantages in competitive districts.

## **How Does it Affect California’s 13th District?**

For the 13th specifically, the effects were significant. Under the proposed map, the 13th adds more registered Democratic voters from Stockton, giving current Democrat Representative Gray a plus-15 edge over Republican opponents. Given the current breakdown of 39.85 percent Democrats versus 30.26 percent Republicans, this would give him a huge boost.

Yet even a bluer map wouldn’t fully settle the contest. Even with the redrawing of the district, the tendencies of the voters in the area is dependent on practical reasons. The district’s battle rooted in water access, farming policies, and economic anxiety, continues, only now with a new map drawn around it.

# Gerrymandering of South Carolina: Is it Racial or Partisan?

## **Racial Gerrymandering in South Carolina?**

To understand South Carolina's current political geography, it helps to know the historical background. After the 15th Amendment granted Black men the right to vote in 1870, Southern states intensified their gerrymandering to dampen their voice. South Carolina was no exception. In 1882, a district in South Carolina that consolidated Black voters was created, enabling all of the other districts to comfortably elect white representatives, despite a large portion of the state population being Black. This segregation of Black voters came to be known as the “Black Seventh” district.

The "Black Seventh" had the desired effect. African American voters made up a numerical majority in five of the seven districts in the state, but they were ‘packed’ into the seventh district to minimize black representation.

And throughout time, race and party affiliation are so closely correlated in South Carolina that the two have become nearly inseparable. And this becomes the central legal question in the state’s most consequential redistricting case in decades.

## **Alexander v. South Carolina NAACP (2024)**

This historical context leads into the recent case of *Alexander v. South Carolina NAACP(2024),* where the prime question of the ruling was whether the 2022 congressional map was a result of racial gerrymandering or partisan.

The case concerned the 1st district of South Carolina, but its implications stretch across the entire state. In 2022, South Carolina adopted a new congressional map. The map moved hundreds of thousands of South Carolinians to different congressional districts, seemingly calibrating the districts’ Black populations to a low enough level to deny equal representation for Black voters. Specifically, more than 30,000 African American voters were moved out of district 1 and into district 6; A classic case of ‘cracking’. South Carolina National Association for the Advancement of Colored People(NAACP) challenged this, arguing that race was the predominant factor for the map redraw.

South Carolina's defense was that it was not racial gerrymandering, but partisan gerrymandering. It was a strategic move relying on the precedent of the *Rucho v. Common Cause (2019)*. For this case, the Supreme Court ruled that partisan gerrymandering cannot be held judiciable in federal court, and the map should stand. The defendants, represented by the president of the South Carolina senate Alexander, asserted that the move to exclude Black voters from District 1 was done as a partisan gerrymander, not a racial one. And the interconnectedness of race and party affiliation in South Carolina made this a difficult case to tackle.

On May 23, 2024, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in South Carolina's favor. The decision reversed the federal trial court's unanimous finding that Congressional District 1 was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander.

The majority concluded that if a map is gerrymandered along partisan lines, the court cannot intervene, even if that causes the map to also be racially gerrymandered. On the other hand, Justice Kagan of the minority chastised the majority for protecting the legislators over thousands of voters, writing: “If calling out a racial gerrymander accuses a state of grave wrong, then so be it.”

## **The 5th district: A Conservative Unity amongst the Storm**

Within this heated disagreement over the redraw of the congressional map, the political tranquility of South Carolina’s 5th district is something to look at. Currently represented by Republican Ralph Norman, South Carolina’s 5th district is the most ideologically aligned district in the US.

South Carolina's 5th district comprises the northern part of the state, bordering North Carolina to the north. It is very similar to the other mostly rural districts in the South. While a chunk of the population lives in the Charlotte metropolitan area, the district is mostly rural. Ethnically, the district is 64.1 percent White and 23.8 percent Black.

York County is the largest county in the district, with almost one-third of its population. And its increasing Republican support has pushed the district as a whole into the Republican column in recent years; In the two most recent presidential elections, the district’s results were 11 percent more Republican than the national average.

It’s notable that before 2010, the district was historically blue. In November 2010, Republican Mick Mulvaney defeated longtime incumbent John Spratt and became the first Republican representative since the 1880s. It overlapped with suburban population growth in the Charlotte metro area, and the redistricting after the 2010 census that further compactified the district around northern suburbs, enhancing its Republican lean. The answer on whether this conservative unity is serendipitous or suspicious remains unclear.
