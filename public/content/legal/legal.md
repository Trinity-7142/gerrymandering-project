# Key Terms
## Gerrymandering
Gerrymandering is the drawing of electoral lines to seek advantage for a specific group, be it a political party, a racial group, or a set of incumbents.

## Malapportionment
Malapportionment was the original method of gerrymandering, involved creating districts of vastly unequal populations, diluting the power of urban voters.

## Voting Rights Act
The Voting Rights act of 1965 was passed by congress to support and uplift the 14th and 15th amendments. 

It aims to prevent anything that leads to a dissolution of minority voting power, and in section 5 established the preclearance clause which meant that states with significant histories of discrimination had to get there redistricting maps approved by the Federal Government prior to going into place. (stopped by ruling of Shelby county) 

## 14th Amendment
The 14th Amendment establishes that no state can deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws. (meant to be created to make a formal principal that all are created equal, especially politically, and in the case of gerrymandering equality must be extended to how individuals are grouped into voting districts)

# Landmark Cases
## Baker v. Carr 369 U.S. 186 186 (1962)
### Context: 
Prior to this case state boundaries were both drawn and redrawn after each census report (every 10 years).
Political Thicket: Regards the Supreme courts removed approach to political gerrymandering and its conflict with the Voting Rights Act. This is when the supreme court use the Equal Protection clause to essentially “solve” gerrymandering or issues with redistricting through the solution of “one person, one vote” in hopes to mandate conflicts between population and voting, to combat urbanization.
WW1 had caused an immense need for rapid manufacturing within the United States. This increase in manufacturing demands caused a shift from the entire state from rural to urban, as people moved into densely populated urban areas to work in factories and industrial plants. This pattern not only occurred in Tennessee but all throughout the state and was a wholistic for the populism period of the United States, causing a complete shift from rural to urban life. By 1962, Tennessee still had not redrawn its legislative districts from post-urbanization.

### What happened?
Charles Barker, a resident of one of Tennessee urban areas sued Joe Carr, a Tennessee state official on the grounds that lack of Tennessee district change since 1901 diluted Barker’s vote. Essentially making a rural district with significantly less people hold the same amount of voting power as a urban district with upwards of 10 times as many people.

### Decision: 
The court rules 6-3 in favor of Barker, and agreed to redistruct under the 1st clause of the 14th Amendement: Equal Protection Clause.
The 1962 Supreme court case of Baker v. Carr ruling gave Federal Courts (the power of choice/granted them the right to intervene) in individual state legislature redistricting maps.

## Reynolds v. Sims 377 U.S. 533 (1964)
### Context:
Alabama’s state legislative districts had not been redrawn since 1901. Population shifts had meant that some rural districts now had barley anyone in comparison to urban districts and upcoming cities, this then created a significant imbalance between the power of individuals votes. Rural voters held vastly more power than those in urban centers.

### What happened?
Voters challenged the malapportionment, as they argued that their votes were being diluted.

### Decision:
8-1 Decision, meaning that state legislative districts must be somewhat equal in population so that votes are weighed the same. This then established the supreme courts “one person, one vote” principal

### Connection to 14th or VRA:
Upheld the Equal Protection Clause, arguing equality for all of the population

## Davis v. Bandemer 478 U.S. 109 (1986)
### Context:
Indiana Democrats challenged a Republican-drawn map, claiming it diluted their votes in violation of the 14th Amendment.

### What happened?
This was the first time the Court took a serious look at whether partisan gerrymandering was something a judge could rule on.

### Decision:
Court ruled that partisan gerrymandering claims are justiciable, which means that courts can hear them (a hearing can take place surrounding that claim/claim you made).

### Impact on gerrymandering (political/racial):
Despite courts now having the ability to hear and essentially have the power to take a stand towards redistricting plans, for around 18 years after Bandemer, courts never found any districting maps to be unconstitutional because there was no specifically set standard or precedent. Which essentially, shifted power that was theoretically in the states hands within the ability to draw the map into the Judges hands within the decisions of the rulings.

## Shaw v. Reno, 509 U.S. 630 (1993)
### Context:
After the 1990 census, North Carolina gained a 12th congressional seat, in order to comply with the Voting Rights Act, the state submitted a proposal with one majority-black district. But the Department of justice rejected this, on the grounds that the state had to have at least two majority black districts for its total population size. To satisfy the demands of the Supreme court North Carolina created District 12 which was a 160 mile long district that was very narrow. Geographically it caused significant amounts of confusion, because despite it being long, it was not much wider than a typical highway.

### What happened?
A group of white voters from North Carolina made the argument that district 12s formation was done through the basis of unconstitutional racial gerrymandering, to which they sued the federal government. What was atypical about there claim was they did not state that there own voting power was diluted due to the district map but they instead argued that because the district was drawn solely on the basis of race it violated the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th amendment because it sanctioned voters into members of racial groups rather then as equal individuals.

### Decision:
5-4 decision, the court held the ruling that although the goal of the Voting Rights Act was to increase the representation of minority groups, minority representation can’t come at the cost of the geographical boundaries that shape districts.

### Connection to 14th or VRA:
Even if the intent of race based redistricting is to help minority groups it’s still not allowed due to the fact that race based classifications are inherently unequal under the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th amendment.

### Impact on gerrymandering (political/racial):
This was a fundamental case, as it paved a new way to challenge the legality of majority-minority districts and it demonstrated that the supreme court would no longer accept the Department of Justice Maximization policies regarding racial minority representation if it resulted in oddly shaped districts.

## Miller v. Johnson (1995)
### Context:
Georgia is a very large state, after the 1990 census Georgias population growth made it entitled to an 11th congressional seat. Georgia as a state had a very large population alongside huge racial discrepancies within populations. Although the state’s population was 27% black, only one of its existing ten districts was a majority minority district. It was ordered thay under the Voting Rights Act (VRA), Georgia had to submit its redistricting plans to get approved through the Department of Justice. The DOJ rejected Georgias first two plans, as they wanted to ensure Georgia had at least three majority-minority districts. Which lead to Georgia drawing the 11th district, which was a massive district that spanned from the Atlantic Coast to Atlanta in order to connect various black neighborhoods.

### What happened?
Certain white voters from the 11th district were upset and they sued, arguing that district 11 was a case of racial gerrymandering and it violated the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th amendment. The claim that they made was because the shape of the district was so irregular the only factor that could have contributed to its shape was race. Both the state of Georgia and the DOJ attempted to defend the map, arguing that its purpose was to comply with the Voting Rights Act and to ensure that there was minority representation throughout the state.

### Decision:
5-4 Ruling, which stated that Georgias 11th District was unconstitutional by the Supreme court

### Impact on gerrymandering (political/racial):
In the majority opinion of the case, Justice Kennedy argued that race can’t be a predominant factor in drawing district lines, meaning it can’t come before other factors or legislature such as compactness, contiguity or political relations. Due to race being in priority to all other factors he argued that this map must be redrawn and that it did not essentially align with the constitution. In the end the Supreme court ruled that the state of Georgia had ignored the traditional districting principals to satisfy the Department of Justice’s want for a specific number of majority-minority districts.
The case itself was centered around the 14th amendments Equal Protection Clause and the Voting Rights Act.
Miller v Johnson continued to develop and strengthen the precedent set in Shaw v. Reno of 1993, and it made it significantly harder for states to shape majority-minority districts for the sake of minority representation, and essentially made it so that race could not be a basis for formation of districts, especially in the case of minorities.

## Vieth v. Jubelirer (2004)
### Context and What happened?
After the 2000 census, PA general assembly adopts a congressional redistricting plan, though the legislature that drew the map was Republican Controlled. The Democrats sued, claiming that the redrawn map was a case of illegal partisan gerrymandering which violated the Equal Protection Clause through the “one person, one vote” principal.

### Decision:
The court was split, this was because 4 justices wanted to declare partisan gerrymandering non-justicable which meant that the courts could not touch it but the swing vote in the end agreed to dismiss the case, although the importance in his dismissal comes with the statement that he refused to say that courts should never intervene.

### Impact on gerrymandering (political/racial):
Demonstrated the conflict inside the court and the general contestation of political gerrymandering.

## Shelby County v. Holder (2013)
### Context:
This case addressed the constitutionality of the "preclearance" system. Under Section 5 of the VRA which declared the ‘covered’ jurisdictions (states like Alabama, Georgia, and Texas with histories of discrimination) had to get federal approval from the Attorney General or a D.C. court before changing voting laws or maps to ensure they didn't harm minority voters. Section 4(b) defined who was covered based on 1960s-era data (literacy tests and low turnout).

### What happened?

Shelby County, Alabama, sued, arguing that while the VRA was necessary in 1965, the formula used to identify them as discriminatory states was outdated and violated their sovereignty.

### Decision:
5-4 decision, courts ruled that Section 4(b) was unconstitutional

### Connection to 14th or VRA:
Through the use of the 14th amendment and the Voting Rights Act, it essentially struck down the coverage formula once used.

### Impact on gerrymandering (political/racial):
Immediately after the ruling, states for example like Texas, enacted restrictive laws that enforced Voter ID which had been previously blocked. Furthermore, it allowed states to redraw maps and change voting procedures without “federal permission.”

## Gill v. Whitford June 18, 2018
### Context and What Happened?
Democrats of Wisconsin challenged Act 43 which was a redistricting plan that they argued was an extreme case of Partisan gerrymandering that violated both the 1st and the 14th amendments, and through the Efficiency Gap which was a metric that was used to illustrate the partisan advantage occurring in Wisconsin.

### Decision:
The use of the specific efficiency standard made many believe that this case would be fundamental in finally setting a formula or standard for partisan gerrymandering. But in the end the supreme court ruled that though there were numbers presented through the tool, the plaintiff (people arguing) did not demonstrate how those numbers hurt specific districts and impacted voting power.

### Impact on gerrymandering (political/racial):
This demonstrated that the court was not eager to put specific legal limits on political gerrymandering or political support. It intensified that racial gerrymandering was clear and conditional violation, while partisan gerrymandering despite many legal fights still stayed as a grey area.
